What is a Preferred Stock?

Preferred stock is a unique type of ownership in a company that blends features of both stocks and bonds. It's designed to offer investors a steadier income stream than common stock, along with a layer of safety, but it comes with its own set of trade-offs. This section explores its core characteristics.

Key Features

  • ✓ Priority Dividends: Holders get paid dividends before common stockholders. These dividends are often a fixed amount.
  • ✓ Liquidation Preference: In case of bankruptcy, preferred stockholders get paid back before common stockholders.
  • ✓ No Voting Rights: Typically, you don't get a say in how the company is run. This is the major trade-off for the preferences you receive.
  • ✓ Limited Growth: The stock price doesn't usually grow as much as common stock. Its value is more tied to its dividend payments.

Cumulative preferred stock requires the company to pay any missed or skipped dividends to preferred shareholders before any dividends can be paid to common shareholders. This offers a significant layer of protection. Non-Cumulative stock does not have this feature; if a dividend is skipped, it is lost forever. Most preferred stock issued by banks is non-cumulative for regulatory reasons.

Convertible preferred stock gives the holder the right to exchange their preferred shares for a predetermined number of the company's common shares. This feature offers the best of both worlds: the stable income of preferred stock with the potential for capital appreciation if the company's common stock price rises significantly.

Callable (or redeemable) preferred stock gives the issuing company the right to buy back the shares from investors at a specified price after a certain date. Companies often do this if interest rates fall, allowing them to refinance their preferred stock at a lower dividend rate. This is a risk for investors, as they may lose a high-yield investment and have to reinvest their money at lower rates.

Participating preferred stock offers investors the chance to receive extra dividends above the normal fixed rate if the company achieves certain financial goals. This allows preferred shareholders to "participate" in the company's success, giving them more upside potential than standard preferred stock, while still providing the safety of a fixed dividend.

The Investor's View: Pros & Cons

Is preferred stock right for your portfolio? It depends on your goals. For income-focused investors, it offers many benefits, but it's not without risks. Here's a balanced look at the advantages and disadvantages.

✔ Pros

  • Higher, Stable Dividends: Generally offers a higher and more predictable income stream than common stock.
  • Safety Net: Priority over common stock in receiving dividends and assets during liquidation provides downside protection.
  • Lower Volatility: Prices tend to be more stable than common stock prices.
  • Tax Advantages: Dividends often qualify for lower tax rates than bond interest in the U.S.

✘ Cons

  • Interest Rate Risk: When interest rates rise, the value of existing preferred shares typically falls.
  • Limited Growth: Lacks the significant capital appreciation potential of common stock.
  • No Say in the Company: The absence of voting rights means you have no influence on corporate decisions.
  • Call Risk: The company may buy back the shares, forcing you to reinvest at potentially lower rates.
